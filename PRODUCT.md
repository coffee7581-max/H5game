# Product

## Register

product

## Users

WePlay serves casual browser-game players aged 20-55, with a roughly balanced gender mix. The primary audience is mobile-first players in Japan, Korea, Taiwan, and Hong Kong, with the United States and Canada as secondary markets. More than two thirds of users are expected to arrive on mobile devices, often looking for a quick game session without installing an app or creating an account.

## Product Purpose

WePlay is a free HTML5 game discovery and play platform with a light community layer. It helps players browse a large game catalog, identify trustworthy and enjoyable games quickly, start playing instantly, and see enough social proof through reviews, activity, and leaderboards to feel that the platform is alive.

## Brand Personality

Playful, social, and trustworthy. The interface should feel like a lively game community rather than a generic AI-generated SaaS page. It should be energetic enough for entertainment, but clear and practical enough for repeat browsing on small screens.

## Anti-references

Avoid obvious AI-template aesthetics, especially blue-purple gradient hero treatments, generic glass cards, over-polished SaaS styling, repetitive identical card grids, and decorative effects that make the interface feel synthetic. The site should not rely on abstract gradients to create personality; the games, community activity, and clear interaction states should carry the experience.

## Design Principles

Prioritize mobile discovery. The most common user should be able to scan, filter, and start a game comfortably on a phone.

Let the catalog be the visual engine. Game art, category cues, ratings, and player signals should create variety, rather than decorative backgrounds.

Make play feel immediate. Primary actions should be obvious, reachable, and consistently labeled across discovery, detail, and play screens.

Build trust through clarity. Reviews, ratings, leaderboards, and game metadata should be readable and credible without overwhelming the page.

Keep energy controlled. Motion and color should support the gaming mood while avoiding loud gradients, low-contrast text, and effects that look automatically generated.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Design mobile-first with large tap targets, readable type, and strong contrast. Aim for WCAG AA contrast for text and controls. Motion should be purposeful, not required for comprehension, and should respect reduced-motion preferences. Avoid relying on color alone to communicate state.
